01
The Name

Everything starts in the basement.

Baizmnt

Inspired by the word Basement — because the real backbone of the entertainment scene lives underground. It’s one thing to attend an event, make memories, and find lifelong companions in a crowd. But immense work went into making that moment happen. Work that lives where no one else sees it: in the basement, where the hosts, organisers, DJs, and crews pour themselves into building something for others to experience.

The name Baizmnt was chosen to do two things simultaneously: acknowledge the unseen labour of everyone who makes nightlife possible, and make that labour easier — for every host, organiser, DJ, photographer, and venue owner who has a great idea but needs a cleaner path to realising it.

The intentional stylisation — dropping the vowels — gives the brand a sharp, modern identity that fits the culture it’s built for. It’s not corporate. It’s not generic. It’s the city’s pulse.

“The real backbone of the entertainment scene is in the underground. The basement. Baizmnt exists to acknowledge that work — and make it easier.”

— Flo Ogbaje, Founder
02
The Problem

A thriving scene held together with tape.

Nigeria’s nightlife and entertainment scene is genuinely vibrant — Lagos especially runs on it. But the infrastructure holding it together is fragmented, unreliable, and built around personal connections rather than accessible systems. Finding out about a rave happening this weekend meant scrolling Instagram stories, asking friends, or hoping a promoter remembered to post. Discovery was word-of-mouth. Booking was who-you-know. Ticketing was one-way.

The deeper you looked, the worse it got. Virtually no one had built a tool that treated every participant in the nightlife ecosystem as a first-class citizen.

🎉
Partygoers

No reliable discovery

Finding quality events meant relying entirely on social media stories, WhatsApp groups, or knowing the right people. FOMO was constant. Real-time info simply didn’t exist.

🎧
DJs

Visibility locked behind relationships

Getting booked meant personally knowing the host or being recommended by someone who did. Upcoming DJs and those new to a city had almost no route to visibility or new gigs.

📋
Organisers

Fragmented operations

Creating an event required juggling separate tools for ticketing, DJ booking, venue sourcing, equipment hire, and promotion. No single platform connected all these needs.

🎟️
Ticket holders

No resale option

Anyone can buy/sell tickets — but if a buyer could no longer attend, there was no legitimate way to resell. Unused tickets were simply lost money.

🏢
Venues & Studios

No unified directory

Venues, studios, and equipment owners had no central place to list availability and reach organisers actively looking to book them. Discoverability was near zero.

📍
Everyone

No real-time situational tools

Once you were out, finding friends, locating an event, or knowing what was happening nearby required a patchwork of separate apps — none of which understood the nightlife context.

03
The Naming Pivot

From Plurr to Baizmnt.

The product didn’t begin with the name Baizmnt. The original name was Plurr — a reference to the rave community acronym for Peace, Love, Unity, Respect, and Responsibility. It felt right. It was culturally grounded, community-forward, and had the right energy for the platform’s vision.

But during the build, market research revealed the name was already taken. Rather than launch under a name with prior ownership, the decision was made to pivot early — before any brand equity had been built, and before any public-facing marketing had gone out.

Plurr

Original concept name.
Name conflict discovered during research.

→
Baizmnt

A stronger identity. Deeper meaning. Entirely original. Built to last.

The pivot turned out to be the better outcome. Baizmnt has a richer conceptual foundation than Plurr ever did — it’s not just a cultural reference, it’s a founding philosophy. The underground metaphor runs through the entire product: visibility for the unseen, tools for the unsung, a stage for everyone who builds from the basement up.

The decision to pivot early rather than late is also worth noting as a product discipline signal. Catching a naming conflict before launch costs almost nothing. After launch, it’s a crisis.

04
The Build

Six months. One person. Every decision.

Baizmnt was built entirely solo — no co-founder, no dedicated designer, no engineering team. Every product decision, every architecture choice, every UI iteration, and every line of code was owned by one person. That constraint forced a level of clarity and prioritisation that a larger team might have diluted.

The approach was deliberate: write the full PRD first to lock in the product vision before a single component was built. Define the users. Define the problems. Define the MVP scope. Only then start building. This product thinking discipline — treating engineering as the execution of a clear strategy rather than the strategy itself — is what allowed a complex, multi-sided platform to ship in a focused, coherent state.

Phase 1
Research & Problem Definition
Mapped the fragmentation across the Nigerian nightlife ecosystem. Identified over six distinct user types and their specific pain points. Validated the gap through personal experience and community observation. Defined the product’s core philosophy: simplification through unification.
Phase 2
PRD & Product Architecture
Wrote a full Product Requirements Document covering goals, user problems, functional requirements, non-functional requirements, user flows, business model, success metrics, risks, and a phased roadmap. This document became the single source of truth for all build decisions.
Phase 3
Naming Pivot: Plurr → Baizmnt
Discovered the original name Plurr was already in use. Made the early pivot decision. Developed the Baizmnt identity — rooted in the basement metaphor — and locked in the brand direction before any public-facing work began.
Key Decision
Phase 4
Design & Technical Architecture
Designed the full UI system: dark-mode first, electric pink and warm gold brand palette, rounded card language, motion principles. Architected the technical stack: React with TypeScript, TailwindCSS, Supabase for auth and real-time data

05
Key Challenges

The hard parts no one sees.

01

Shipping a multi-sided platform alone

Baizmnt serves more than six distinct user types simultaneously — partygoers, DJs, organisers, venue owners, equipment providers, vendors, those simply seeking community, and more. Each user type has different needs, different flows, and different trust requirements. Designing and building a coherent experience for all of them without a team meant ruthless prioritisation: what does each user need to accomplish in their first session, and how do I make that frictionless?

02

Technical architecture for a real-time ecosystem

An event discovery platform lives and dies by its real-time data. The live map, the “happening now” indicators, the beacon feature, and the ticketing inventory all require real-time state. Architecting Supabase to handle concurrent reads across these features, while keeping the UI performant and the UX smooth, required deliberate data modelling decisions that couldn’t be undone later.

03

Booking & marketplace logic

A booking flow is not just a form. It involves availability state, approval workflows, payment handling, and confirmation logic — all of which need to work correctly the first time. Building this for DJs, venues, studios, and equipment simultaneously meant abstracting the core booking pattern into something reusable without sacrificing the specific UX each category needed.

04

The naming pivot mid-build

Discovering a naming conflict mid-development on a product you’ve been mentally calling by one name for months is disorienting. The decision to pivot immediately rather than proceed and deal with it later was the right call — but it required rebuilding the brand identity, reconsidering the product positioning, and finding a name with even more conceptual depth. Baizmnt emerged stronger for it.

06
The Product

One platform. Every role.

The central design principle of Baizmnt is unification without complexity. Every feature that exists on the platform is there because a real person in the nightlife ecosystem had a problem that no existing tool was solving well. The product’s job is to disappear — to make the process so smooth that the user never has to think about the platform, only the experience they’re moving toward.

🗺️
Discovery

Live City Map & Event Cards

Users open Baizmnt and immediately see what’s happening in their city in real time. Every event card carries a Get Directions button that opens Google Maps navigation. No searching. No scrolling through Instagram stories. The city’s pulse, at a glance.

🎟️
Ticketing

Buy, Store & Resell

Tickets purchased on Baizmnt can be resold on Baizmnt. If a user can’t attend or simply changes their mind, the resell feature puts their tickets back on the marketplace to be purchased by other users who need them — a peer-to-peer system that didn’t exist in this market before Baizmnt.

🎧
Booking

DJ & Creative Marketplace

Baizmnt AI creates portfolios or EPKs for DJs on the fly. These can then be shared with organisers upon request. Upcoming DJs get the same visibility as established ones. And with the seamless communication Baizmnt provides, photographers and other creatives get the visibility they require for their craft— the playing field is the quality of output, not who you know.

📡
Safety & Social

Beacon Feature

On a night out, friends can find eachother on the live map using the built-in beacon feature on Baizmnt. A real-time safety and social layer built specifically for the unpredictability of nightlife environments.

🏢
Marketplace

Venues, Studios & Equipment

Organisers planning an event can source their DJ, their venue, their stage equipment, their lighting rig, and their photographer all within one platform. Every operational need an event host has, addressed in one place. A never-before-seen level of integration.

🛍️
Extended Marketplace

Merch & Vendors

The marketplace extends beyond event logistics to merchandise and vendor listings — expanding Baizmnt’s role from event discovery tool to a full commerce layer for the nightlife economy.

📋
Organiser Tools

End-to-End Event Creation & Management

Organisers publish events with full detail: flyer, lineup, set times, ticket tiers, dress code, parking, rules, and directions. They track sales, manage comments, and update event info in real time. Everything an organiser needs from idea to night-of, in one workflow. No spreadsheets. No WhatsApp coordination threads. No separate ticketing link.

08
What’s Next

Phase 2 and the community vision.

The MVP is live and the ecosystem is forming. Phase 2 is about deepening the product’s value in three directions: smarter discovery, richer social infrastructure, and the feature I’m most excited about — Community.

The goal isn’t just an app where people find events. The goal is a platform where the partygoer, the DJ, the organiser, and the photographer interact with each other naturally — where the lines between user types blur into a genuine nightlife community. That cross-communication between people who onboarded for entirely different reasons is what creates an ecosystem that can’t be replicated by any single-use tool.

09
Reflection

What building Baizmnt taught me.

Building a multi-sided platform solo is an education that no job can fully replicate. You make every decision with incomplete information, under real constraints, with real consequences. There’s no one to defer to and no one to blame when something doesn’t work. That accountability sharpens everything — your scoping instincts, your architecture choices, your willingness to cut a feature to ship on time.

The thing I’m most proud of about Baizmnt isn’t any individual feature. It’s the ecosystem. The fact that a DJ and an event organiser and a partygoer can all open the same app and each find something built specifically for them — and then find each other — is the hardest kind of product problem to solve. It requires thinking about users not as isolated personas but as participants in a shared economy.

I built Baizmnt because the gap was real and no one else was filling it. I built it the way I did — with a PRD, a clear philosophy, deliberate tooling choices, and a long build rather than a quick one — because the nightlife community deserves a product built with genuine care. The basement crowd always has.

“Simplicity is the main idea. Putting everything together in a way that works without hassle for the user — that’s the goal. It took real thought to build and requires continuous work to maintain.”

— Flo Ogbaje, on what she’s most proud of
